# 1. Executive summary

## 1.1 The organization

NOVA Performing Arts is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization developing accessible, high-quality performing arts experiences in Central Texas. NOVA 8 Percussion is its first major youth instructional program.

NOVA 8 is a noncompetitive, ensemble-centered percussion program that gives young musicians more time to grow. It provides serious instruction outside the limits of a school marching season while deliberately supporting, and never displacing, school music programs.

The model separates **student access** from **school cooperation**. Students and families may discover, apply to, and enroll in NOVA 8 on their own. Educator referrals are welcomed but are never required. School cooperation is an operating commitment: NOVA 8 respects school schedules and student obligations, teaches transferable skills, avoids unauthorized school-specific material, and communicates with an individual student's director only with family consent. Formal school partnerships deepen that cooperation but do not control access to the program.

The name refers to the final count before an ensemble begins: “5, 6, 5, 6, 7, 8.” That last count represents readiness, shared attention, and the moment before action.

> **Named for the final count before an ensemble begins, NOVA 8 prepares young musicians for what comes next.**

## 1.2 The opportunity

Young percussionists often develop within compressed school calendars. Directors must prepare entire ensembles for performances and contests while balancing fundamentals, repertoire, movement, equipment, and many different student readiness levels. Access to percussion specialists, rehearsal time, and equipment also varies across schools.

NOVA 8 addresses this gap through:

- Eight-week student programs that develop transferable percussion and ensemble skills
- An 8th Grade Intensive that prepares students for the transition into high-school percussion
- Ticketed social clinics open to the public
- Percussion Playground, a signature participatory ensemble experience
- Specialty clinics and workshops
- A supervised Teaching Fellowship for emerging percussion educators
- Cooperative relationships with school directors, collegiate studios, independent ensembles, hosts, and community partners

### How participation and partnership fit together

| Question | NOVA 8 policy |
|---|---|
| Who may apply? | Any student or family within the program's age, readiness, and practical service range |
| Is a director referral required? | No. Referrals are one optional way students may learn about or enter the program |
| Does a director approve enrollment? | No. NOVA 8 makes placement and capacity decisions directly with the family |
| Must the student's school partner with NOVA 8? | No. Students from non-partner schools have the same access to open-enrollment programs |
| What does school cooperation mean? | NOVA 8 respects school commitments, develops transferable skills, avoids conflicting recruitment, and seeks educator input at the program level |
| When may NOVA contact a student's director? | Only when the family authorizes student-specific communication, except where disclosure is legally required |

## 1.3 The operating thesis

NOVA 8 will succeed if families can access it easily, school programs experience it as supportive, educators find it credible, and supporters understand how the model is financed.

The student program has two equally valid enrollment paths:

1. **Direct enrollment:** A student or family finds NOVA 8 through a clinic, the website, community outreach, or word of mouth and applies directly.
2. **Educator referral:** A director or instructor shares the opportunity or recommends it to a family. The family still chooses whether to apply, and NOVA 8 handles placement and enrollment.

Neither path gives a school or director control over access. Formal school relationships improve alignment, trust, facilities, and reach, but the open-enrollment program remains available without them.

The model combines earned and contributed revenue. Tuition and tickets demonstrate demand and help pay for delivery. Donations, sponsorships, grants, institutional fees, and in-kind space keep tuition accessible, support scholarships, and fund the instructional quality that tuition alone would not sustain at the intended cohort size.

## 1.4 Founding goals

During the first operating year, NOVA 8 will aim to:

- Establish an educator advisory group and at least three active school relationships
- Secure a reliable rehearsal site
- Launch the first eight-week cycle in November 2026
- Serve 40 core-program seat-enrollments and 18 students in an 8th Grade Intensive
- Present at least six public social clinics and two Percussion Playground events
- Pilot the NOVA 8 Teaching Fellowship with two to four fellows
- Provide need-based assistance without lowering instructional standards
- Produce evidence that students return to their school ensembles better prepared
- Finish the year with positive unrestricted operating cash and a documented model for renewal

“Seat-enrollment” means one registration in one program cycle. Returning students may account for more than one seat-enrollment.

# 2. Organization, mission, and identity

## 2.1 Relationship to NOVA Performing Arts

NOVA Performing Arts is the legal, fiduciary, and governance home of NOVA 8. The parent organization will hold contracts, receive charitable contributions, maintain insurance, oversee financial controls, adopt youth-protection policies, and supervise program leadership.

NOVA 8 is a program and brand of NOVA Performing Arts, not a separate legal entity.

## 2.2 Mission

> **NOVA 8 gives young percussionists more time to grow through accessible, high-quality ensemble training that strengthens their contribution to school and community ensembles.**

## 2.3 Vision

NOVA 8 envisions a Central Texas percussion community in which:

- Students can develop serious skills without joining a competitive circuit
- Students and families can enroll directly, whether or not their school has a relationship with NOVA 8
- Incoming high-school percussionists feel prepared rather than overwhelmed
- School directors can refer students to a trusted complementary program
- Students from differently resourced schools can access excellent instruction and instruments
- Emerging educators can learn to teach through supervised practice
- Families, donors, and community members can experience ensemble percussion directly
- Program success is measured by growth, belonging, musicianship, and usefulness to school ensembles

## 2.4 Brand promise

**NOVA 8 Percussion. More time to grow.**

The promise is both practical and emotional. Students receive more instructional time, more space to learn, and more chances to become confident ensemble members. School programs receive students who have developed fundamentals and rehearsal habits that can transfer back to their own ensembles.

## 2.5 What NOVA 8 is

- Noncompetitive
- Ensemble-centered
- Skills-based
- Cooperative with school programs
- Open to direct family enrollment
- Serious but supportive
- Accessible by design
- Public-facing as well as student-serving
- A teaching environment for both performers and emerging educators

## 2.6 What NOVA 8 is not

- A replacement for a school band or percussion program
- A recruiting pipeline away from school ensembles
- A competitive indoor percussion organization
- A private-lesson studio presented as an ensemble
- A venue for teaching a school’s show material without the director’s approval
- A program that equates exclusivity with quality
- A program that requires director referral, approval, or school affiliation for open enrollment
- An equipment acquisition project

# 3. Defining operating principles

## 3.1 Open access and school-respecting cooperation

Students and families may enter NOVA 8 directly. Director referral, approval, and formal school participation are not admissions requirements. NOVA 8 makes placement decisions using published age, readiness, capacity, safety, and instrumentation criteria.

At the same time, NOVA 8 will design curriculum, recruitment, scheduling, communication, and evaluation to respect school music programs and, where relationships exist, in cooperation with their educators. Participation should strengthen a student’s commitment and contribution to the student’s own school ensemble. Cooperation governs NOVA 8's conduct; it does not gate student access.

## 3.2 Growth before competition

NOVA 8 will create ambitious ensemble experiences without rankings, placements, or contest outcomes. Standards remain high, but the reason to improve is musicianship and shared performance rather than competitive standing.

## 3.3 Transferable learning

Instruction will emphasize technique, timing, reading, movement, listening, rehearsal discipline, leadership, and equipment care. These skills should transfer across schools, instruments, repertoires, and future ensembles.

## 3.4 Access with dignity

Financial aid will be simple, private, and respectful. Students receiving assistance will participate fully. NOVA 8 will seek underwriting for access rather than lowering program quality.

## 3.5 Excellent teaching is a learned practice

The Teaching Fellowship will treat instruction as a discipline. Fellows will receive defined responsibilities, observation, feedback, and supervised progression instead of being used merely as inexpensive staffing.

## 3.6 Safety before convenience

Youth protection, appropriate communication, screening, attendance controls, emergency preparation, and clear adult boundaries will be built into operations before enrollment opens.

## 3.7 Sustainable growth

NOVA 8 will add cohorts, sites, and program types only when leadership, facilities, safety, instructional quality, and cash flow can support them.

## 3.8 Evidence over assumption

The founding year is a structured pilot. NOVA 8 will collect feedback and outcome data, publish an annual impact summary, and adjust programs based on what students, families, educators, and financial results show.

# 4. Community need and opportunity

## 4.1 Student need

Percussion students must coordinate sound production, rhythm, reading, movement, listening, equipment, and visual awareness. Those demands are difficult to develop when rehearsal time is devoted primarily to immediate performance needs.

Common student needs include:

- More repetition on fundamentals without the pressure of an approaching contest
- Stronger music-reading and timing confidence
- Experience listening across battery and front ensemble roles
- Better physical coordination and movement fundamentals
- A gradual introduction to high-school rehearsal expectations
- Access to instruments that are impractical for families to own
- A meaningful ensemble experience for students who do not want a competitive commitment
- A pathway to continue developing between school-based marching seasons

## 4.2 School and director need

School programs vary in specialist staffing, equipment, enrollment, facilities, and available rehearsal time. Even strong programs may be unable to provide every student with extended individual attention during a compressed season.

NOVA 8 can increase instructional capacity by:

- Reinforcing needs identified by directors
- Providing additional ensemble time
- Offering transition preparation for incoming ninth graders
- Hosting focused clinics that a school could not efficiently run alone
- Providing supervised emerging instructors
- Creating a regional option for students from programs too small to field every percussion role
- Returning students with stronger technique, confidence, and rehearsal habits

## 4.3 Emerging teacher need

College percussionists and advanced performers may enter student teaching or paid instruction with strong performance backgrounds but limited supervised teaching experience. NOVA 8 can provide a functioning educational setting in which they learn to plan, communicate, diagnose, pace, and lead under an experienced educator.

## 4.4 Community need

Marching percussion is highly visible in performance but often inaccessible as a participatory experience. Public social clinics can invite families, adults, prospective students, potential hosts, and supporters to make music together. This creates community value and a natural introduction to NOVA 8.

## 4.5 Regional opportunity

Travis County’s July 2025 estimated population was 1,389,670, with 19.7 percent under age 18, or approximately 274,000 young people. The practical NOVA 8 market extends beyond Travis County into Williamson, Hays, Bastrop, and surrounding communities, but weekly enrollment will depend more on travel time, direct family demand, public-event conversion, and educator awareness than on county boundaries.

Texas Education Agency enrollment data show multiple large middle and high schools across the Austin-area districts. This does not establish the number of percussion students, but it confirms a substantial school-based audience. Launch validation should combine family-facing demand tests with district-by-district educator discovery rather than relying on either route alone.

NOVA 8 should not claim a precise serviceable market until it completes 2026 family and educator discovery. The useful launch question is narrower: can direct family interest, public events, and cooperative educator relationships together reliably fill one 20-student cohort? No single channel is a prerequisite. The founding plan is designed to identify the combination that works.

# 5. Theory of change

## 5.1 Core proposition

If NOVA 8 provides young percussionists with additional, well-sequenced ensemble instruction in cooperation with school educators, then students will develop stronger fundamentals, confidence, listening, rehearsal habits, and readiness. Those gains will benefit both the students and the school ensembles to which they return.

If NOVA 8 simultaneously trains emerging teachers under experienced supervision, it will also strengthen the future instructional workforce available to Central Texas music programs.

## 5.2 Inputs, activities, outputs, and outcomes

| Stage | NOVA 8 model |
|---|---|
| Inputs | Instruments, electronics, educators, fellows, rehearsal space, curriculum, families, optional school partners, safety systems, funding, administration |
| Activities | Eight-week rehearsals, 8th Grade Intensive, public clinics, Percussion Playground, assessments, fellow mentoring, demonstrations |
| Immediate outputs | Students served, instructional hours, clinic tickets, fellow teaching hours, school relationships, scholarships |
| Near-term outcomes | Improved technique, reading, timing, ensemble awareness, confidence, attendance, rehearsal behavior, and teacher readiness |
| Longer-term outcomes | Better-prepared school musicians, stronger school programs, recurring student pathways, effective emerging educators, durable community support |

## 5.3 Conditions for success

- Directors view NOVA 8 as an ally
- The lead educator has both artistic credibility and youth-teaching skill
- Rehearsal space is reliable and geographically practical
- Families understand the eight-week commitment
- Tuition is accessible without leaving the program structurally underfunded
- Fellows receive genuine supervision
- Public clinics feel welcoming, social, and distinctive
- Evaluation is light enough to operate but strong enough to guide decisions

# 6. Program portfolio

NOVA 8 will operate as a connected family of offerings. Public events introduce the experience, eight-week programs provide sustained development, the 8th Grade Intensive addresses a clear transition need, and advanced or returning students receive an ongoing pathway.

Open-enrollment offerings are available directly to students and families. A family may begin with an interest form, a public clinic, or an eight-week program application without contacting a school director. Educator referral is an optional discovery and support channel, not a separate class of admission.

## 6.1 Eight-week NOVA 8 programs

### Purpose

The eight-week program is the central student offering. It gives students enough time to develop habits and ensemble awareness while remaining shorter and more accessible than a competitive season.

### Founding format

| Element | Planning assumption |
|---|---|
| Session length | Eight instructional weeks |
| Rehearsal frequency | Once weekly |
| Rehearsal duration | 2.5 to 3 hours |
| Cohort size | 16 to 24 students, budget based on 20 |
| Primary ages | Middle and high school, grouped by readiness |
| Instruments | Battery and front ensemble, based on staffing and enrollment |
| Culmination | Demonstration, informal performance, or family sharing |
| Student feedback | Mid-cycle note and end-of-cycle next-step recommendation |

### Program levels

Initial levels should remain simple:

1. **Foundations:** Developing technique, reading, timing, rehearsal habits, and first ensemble awareness.
2. **Ensemble:** Students with established fundamentals who are ready for greater independence, listening, movement, and musical responsibility.
3. **8th Grade Intensive:** A transition-focused cohort described below.

NOVA 8 should add an advanced label only when recurring enrollment supports a genuinely distinct curriculum. Placement is a teaching decision, not a prestige ranking.

### Eight-week progression

| Week | Instructional focus |
|---:|---|
| 1 | Orientation, safety, baseline assessment, sound and pulse |
| 2 | Technique consistency, subdivision, rehearsal vocabulary |
| 3 | Reading, timing relationships, basic ensemble listening |
| 4 | Movement or physical coordination, role awareness, first integration |
| 5 | Dynamic control, balance, recovery from errors, mid-cycle feedback |
| 6 | Musical responsibility, transitions, student-led repetitions |
| 7 | Full-ensemble continuity, communication, performance preparation |
| 8 | Culminating demonstration, reflection, feedback, next-step recommendation |

The specific repertoire and instrument assignments will be selected by the artistic lead in consultation with partner educators.

## 6.2 8th Grade Intensive

### Purpose

The 8th Grade Intensive prepares students for the transition from middle-school percussion into high-school expectations. It is not an audition camp and does not pre-teach a high school’s show. It develops transferable readiness.

### Objectives

Students should leave able to:

- Produce a more consistent sound with efficient technique
- Read and count common rhythmic vocabulary with greater confidence
- Understand basic battery and front ensemble roles
- Follow high-school-style rehearsal instructions
- Mark music and prepare assigned material
- Move with pulse and spatial awareness at a developmentally appropriate level
- Care for instruments and participate in efficient setup and teardown
- Receive correction constructively and recover from mistakes
- Enter a new ensemble with realistic expectations and lower anxiety

### Format

- Eight weekly sessions, or a concentrated school-break version after the core model is proven
- 16 to 20 students
- Initial readiness assessment
- Family orientation
- Optional director input where the student comes from a partner school and the family authorizes it
- Final demonstration for families and invited educators
- Short progress summary for the family, with sharing to the school director only with appropriate consent

### Cooperative safeguard

The Intensive will not imitate or override a partner school’s pedagogy. Before launch, NOVA 8 will ask directors what incoming students most need and what approaches or terminology should be aligned.

## 6.3 Public social clinics

### Purpose

Ticketed social clinics invite the public into participatory percussion. They build awareness, produce earned revenue, identify prospective students, cultivate hosts and supporters, and create a community around the organization.

### Potential formats

- Family percussion sessions
- Adult beginner ensemble nights
- “Play in the Ensemble” experiences
- Community rhythm events
- Student-and-parent clinics
- Section discovery sessions
- Guest artist workshops
- Leadership and section-leader clinics
- Educator and college-student teaching labs

### Operating characteristics

- Single-event registration
- Instruments provided
- Limited enrollment
- Clear age and experience guidance
- No prior experience required for selected events
- Participant and observer tickets where appropriate
- Sponsored or subsidized tickets available
- A social, participatory atmosphere rather than a recital or sales presentation
- A brief invitation to continue with NOVA 8 after the event

## 6.4 Percussion Playground

Percussion Playground is the signature public experience and the primary experiential introduction to NOVA 8.

### Founding model

- Approximately 20 participants
- A school gym, band hall, or comparable large rehearsal space
- A simulated ensemble environment
- Guided experience across instruments or ensemble roles
- Sufficient structure for participants to make something together
- Accessible to prospective hosts, families, adults, donors, and community members
- Approximately 90 to 120 minutes, followed by informal conversation
- Instruments provided
- Participant and observer tickets
- Optional presentation sponsor or host underwriter

The experience should feel like entering the energy and coordination of an ensemble, not rotating through disconnected instrument demonstrations.

## 6.5 Specialty clinics

Once the founding cycle is stable, NOVA 8 may offer:

- Audition preparation
- Battery fundamentals
- Front ensemble fundamentals
- Cymbal technique
- Movement for percussionists
- Reading and timing labs
- Leadership and section-leader development
- Guest artist workshops
- School-requested clinics

Specialty clinics should be added in response to partner or student demand, not merely to fill a calendar.

## 6.6 Culminating demonstrations and performances

Every sustained program should end with a meaningful act of sharing. The format may be informal and should emphasize student growth, ensemble responsibility, and what was learned.

Possible settings include:

- Family demonstrations
- Partner-school showcases
- Community events
- Sponsor gatherings
- Joint clinics with school ensembles
- Donor previews

NOVA 8 will avoid turning a noncompetitive program into a performance-pressure program. The culminating event serves learning and community, not spectacle alone.

## 6.7 Program pathway

| Entry point | Next step | Continuing pathway |
|---|---|---|
| Direct family or student inquiry | Readiness and placement process | Eight-week program, Intensive, or appropriate clinic |
| Public social clinic | Eight-week program or another clinic | Returning student, family supporter, volunteer, or host |
| Percussion Playground | Program inquiry or sponsorship | Student enrollment, host relationship, or donor cultivation |
| Educator referral | Family chooses whether to apply; NOVA 8 handles placement | Return to school ensemble stronger; individual feedback shared only with consent |
| 8th Grade Intensive | High-school participation and later NOVA 8 cycle | Leadership or advanced ensemble opportunity |
| Core eight-week program | Return cycle or specialty clinic | Student leadership, alumni, or future teaching pathway |
| Collegiate performer | Teaching Fellowship | More advanced teaching responsibility or partner-school work |

# 7. Cooperation with school music programs

School cooperation is not a secondary partnership strategy. It is an operating ethic that shapes how NOVA 8 schedules, teaches, recruits, and communicates. It does not mean that schools or directors control individual access.

## 7.1 Participation without school involvement

A student may participate even when the student's school has no relationship with NOVA 8 and the director has no involvement. In that case:

- The family applies and registers directly with NOVA 8
- NOVA 8 assesses readiness and makes placement decisions
- The family identifies known school commitments so scheduling conflicts can be avoided
- NOVA 8 teaches transferable material and reinforces the student's responsibility to the school ensemble
- NOVA 8 does not contact the director about the student without family consent
- The student receives the same instruction, aid consideration, and progression opportunities as a student from a partner school

Direct enrollment and school cooperation therefore coexist. The first protects access; the second protects trust and educational alignment.

## 7.2 School partnership pledge

NOVA 8 will:

- Encourage and reinforce student commitment to school ensembles
- Recruit transparently through both direct family outreach and optional director referrals
- Avoid conflicts with school rehearsals, performances, auditions, camps, and competitive seasons
- Ask directors what developmental needs would be most useful to address
- Teach transferable skills rather than school-specific show material unless invited and authorized
- Never present itself as a superior alternative to a student’s school program
- Never recruit students into an activity that requires abandoning a school commitment
- Share appropriate progress information only with consent and clear purpose
- Credit partner schools and directors appropriately
- Invite school partners to evaluate whether NOVA 8 is useful
- Never imply that a director must approve a student's participation in an open-enrollment offering

## 7.3 Partnership levels

The following levels describe organizational relationships, not student eligibility. A student may enroll whether the school is at any of these levels or has no relationship with NOVA 8.

### Level 1: Referral partner

The director receives clear program information, refers students when appropriate, and may provide general input on needs and scheduling. No facility or financial commitment is required.

### Level 2: Host partner

The school or district provides rehearsal or clinic space under a written facility agreement. NOVA 8 may provide a clinic, reserved seats, staff development value, or other agreed benefit in return.

### Level 3: Instructional partner

NOVA 8 and the school coordinate a specific student cohort, clinic, transition program, or teaching-fellow placement. The agreement defines schedule, curriculum boundaries, communication, student data, cost, safety, equipment, and evaluation.

### Level 4: Strategic school or district partner

The partner helps shape recurring programming across multiple campuses or years. This may include a service contract, shared recruitment, designated facilities, educator advisory participation, and jointly defined outcomes.

## 7.4 What NOVA 8 provides to school partners

- Specialized percussion instruction
- Additional ensemble time
- Instruments and electronics for NOVA activities
- Program administration and registration
- Screened and trained instructional staff
- A supervised teaching-fellow structure
- Transition preparation for incoming high-school students
- Clinics aligned to identified needs
- Appropriate progress summaries and program-level outcome reporting
- Public experiences that can introduce families and supporters to percussion

## 7.5 What school partners may provide

- Student referrals
- Input on student and program needs
- Calendar coordination
- Family communication or approved flyer distribution
- Rehearsal or event space
- A designated liaison
- Equipment access where mutually agreed
- Feedback on student transfer and program usefulness
- Participation in an educator advisory group

## 7.6 Director communication cycle

This cycle applies only where a school relationship exists or a family requests director involvement. Direct student enrollment does not automatically create a director communication process.

1. **Before enrollment:** Make program purpose, dates, curriculum boundaries, tuition, aid, and both enrollment paths clear. Partner educators may share or refer, but families enroll directly with NOVA 8.
2. **Before placement:** Invite general recommendations or specific student referrals. Obtain family consent before discussing identifiable student needs.
3. **During the cycle:** Provide only necessary operational updates. Avoid creating additional administrative work for directors.
4. **After the cycle:** Offer a concise program report and, with consent, a student progress summary.
5. **Annual review:** Ask whether participating students returned more prepared and whether the program should change.

## 7.7 Calendar coordination

NOVA 8 will maintain a regional conflict calendar that includes:

- School rehearsals and performances
- District and region events
- Audition cycles
- Marching band camps
- UIL marching contests
- Indoor percussion commitments
- School breaks and testing periods

The 2026 UIL State Open Class Marching Band Contests run November 2 through November 11. The recommended founding-cycle start is therefore Saturday, November 14, subject to local director input. This protects the stated cooperative principle and avoids asking students or educators to divide attention during the final state-contest period.

## 7.8 Data and consent boundaries

NOVA 8 will not create informal backchannels about students. A family's disclosure of the student's school does not itself authorize NOVA 8 to contact the director. Any exchange of identifiable student information must have a defined educational purpose, appropriate family consent, and secure handling. Program-level feedback should be preferred when individual detail is unnecessary.

## 7.9 Partner evaluation question

The central school-partner measure is simple:

> Did NOVA 8 return students to their school programs more prepared, confident, responsible, and capable of contributing to the ensemble?

# 8. Educational model

## 8.1 Instructional principles

- Fundamentals support expression
- Ensemble awareness is taught, not assumed
- Students need clear expectations and psychologically safe correction
- Repetition should be purposeful
- Responsibility increases with readiness
- Feedback should identify a next action
- High standards and student dignity are compatible
- Curriculum should transfer across programs

## 8.2 Curriculum domains

| Domain | Examples of learning |
|---|---|
| Sound production | Grip, stroke, touch, implement choice, consistency, tone |
| Rhythm and timing | Pulse, subdivision, interpretation, timing relationships, recovery |
| Reading | Counting, notation, markings, form, preparation habits |
| Ensemble listening | Balance, alignment, role, vertical and horizontal awareness |
| Movement | Posture, pulse, coordination, spatial awareness, efficiency |
| Rehearsal practice | Attention, response, marking, repetition, setup, teardown |
| Musicianship | Phrasing, dynamics, articulation, intention, communication |
| Responsibility | Attendance, preparation, equipment care, peer contribution |
| Leadership | Clear communication, demonstration, listening, accountability |

## 8.3 Placement

Placement will be designed to help students succeed. It may include:

- A family and student readiness questionnaire
- School experience and instrument background
- A short low-pressure playing or reading activity
- Student goals
- Director referral information, with consent
- Accessibility or support needs

Students who are not ready for a particular cohort should receive a clear alternative, such as Foundations, a clinic, private instruction referral, or a later cycle.

## 8.4 Student assessment

Assessment will be concise and developmental.

### Baseline

- Current technique and sound
- Pulse and subdivision
- Reading confidence
- Rehearsal response
- Ensemble awareness
- Student goal

### Mid-cycle

- One demonstrated strength
- One priority for the remaining weeks
- Attendance and preparation concern, if any
- Adjustment to instrument or role, if useful

### End of cycle

- Growth observed
- Current readiness
- Recommended next step
- Student reflection
- Family feedback
- Optional director feedback with consent

## 8.5 Attendance and completion

The educational value of an eight-week program depends on continuity. Families will be asked to commit to all dates before registering. A completion benchmark of at least seven of eight rehearsals is recommended, with reasonable exceptions for illness, school obligations, family emergencies, and access needs.

## 8.6 Repertoire and intellectual property

NOVA 8 will use original, licensed, commissioned, or appropriately permitted educational material. Repertoire should serve curriculum and fit the actual cohort. NOVA will not use a school’s current or planned show material without written permission.

## 8.7 Accessibility

NOVA 8 will seek reasonable participation solutions for students with disabilities or differing learning needs. Registration should invite families to identify accommodations without requiring unnecessary medical disclosure. Facility accessibility, sensory load, hearing protection, mobility, communication, and emergency procedures should be considered before placement.

# 9. Teaching Fellowship and institutional partnerships

## 9.1 Purpose

The NOVA 8 Teaching Fellowship gives selected collegiate and advanced percussionists structured, mentored experience teaching young musicians. It also expands student access to more individual feedback without substituting unsupervised labor for professional instruction.

## 9.2 Fellow experience

Fellows will receive:

- Orientation to NOVA 8 mission and school-partnership principles
- Youth-protection training and required screening
- Defined teaching assignments
- Observation of an experienced lead educator
- A gradual increase in responsibility
- Lesson and rehearsal planning practice
- Regular feedback
- Reflection on communication, pacing, diagnosis, and leadership
- Documented teaching hours and a completion evaluation

## 9.3 Progression of responsibility

| Stage | Typical responsibility |
|---|---|
| Observe | Watch the lead educator and discuss instructional choices |
| Assist | Support setup, demonstrations, attendance, and individual feedback |
| Lead a segment | Teach a warm-up, exercise, or short section block |
| Plan and teach | Prepare a supervised instructional segment |
| Integrate | Lead a larger rehearsal portion with feedback |

No fellow should be left solely responsible for a youth cohort unless policy, qualifications, staffing, and supervision explicitly permit it.

## 9.4 Institutional instructional partnerships

Potential partners include:

- University percussion studios
- Music education programs
- Independent ensembles
- Professional and community music organizations
- School districts and campuses

NOVA provides a functioning youth ensemble, experienced supervision, curriculum structure, instruments, scheduling, administration, safety procedures, and meaningful teaching assignments.

The institutional partner may provide candidate referrals, a faculty or artistic liaison, mentoring, curriculum consultation, rehearsal space, recruitment assistance, equipment access, or financial support.

## 9.5 Partnership value proposition

For an institutional partner, the fellowship converts broad interest in teaching into documented supervised practice. For NOVA 8, it develops an educator pipeline and connects the program to a wider instructional community. For school partners, it can increase the number of emerging instructors who understand responsible, cooperative school-based teaching.

## 9.6 Fellowship funding

Fellows should receive stipends when resources permit. Fellowship costs may be supported through:

- Named sponsors
- University or ensemble partnership funds
- Education grants
- Individual donors
- Institutional service agreements

# 10. Market definition and competitive position

## 10.1 Primary geographic market

The initial weekly-service area is greater Austin and surrounding Central Texas communities. Practical recruitment should be based on a 30 to 45 minute family travel radius from the rehearsal site, not a fixed county boundary.

Single-day clinics and Percussion Playground can draw from a wider radius because they require less recurring travel.

## 10.2 Primary student segments

1. Middle-school percussionists who need stronger fundamentals and ensemble experience
2. Eighth graders preparing for high-school participation
3. High-school students seeking additional development outside immediate school performance demands
4. Students from programs with limited specialist instruction or instrumentation
5. Students seeking serious noncompetitive ensemble participation
6. Returning NOVA 8 students ready for deeper responsibility

## 10.3 Public-event segments

- Prospective students and families
- Adults interested in trying percussion
- Music educators and college students
- Potential donors, sponsors, and hosts
- Community groups
- Corporate teams seeking a distinctive participatory event

## 10.4 Customer, participant, and beneficiary

The student is the primary beneficiary. The family is usually the purchaser and may enroll directly. School educators are valuable referral, alignment, and trust partners, but they are not admissions gatekeepers. Donors and sponsors subsidize access and quality. Hosts provide space or audiences. Institutional partners support educator development.

Marketing and program design should recognize these different roles rather than treating everyone as a generic customer.

## 10.5 Alternatives and competitors

| Alternative | Primary value | Limitation relative to NOVA 8 | NOVA 8 relationship |
|---|---|---|---|
| School percussion program | Core educational home and ensemble identity | Limited time and variable specialist capacity | Essential partner, never a competitor |
| Private lessons | Individualized technique and repertoire | Limited full-ensemble experience | Complementary referral relationship |
| Competitive indoor ensemble | High-intensity performance and competition | Larger time, cost, audition, and travel commitment | Different student need and value proposition |
| Summer camp | Concentrated learning and social experience | Short duration and limited continuity | Complementary seasonal option |
| Independent clinic | Focused specialist access | Usually isolated from a progression pathway | Potential partner or feeder experience |
| Community ensemble | Recurring group participation | May not focus on youth marching-percussion development | Adjacent collaborator |

## 10.6 Differentiation

NOVA 8 is differentiated by the combination of:

- A formal commitment to school-program cooperation
- Noncompetitive ensemble training
- Eight-week cycles instead of a full competitive season
- A specific middle-to-high-school transition offering
- Instruments supplied
- Public ticketed social experiences
- A supervised educator-development pathway
- A nonprofit revenue model designed for access
- Evaluation based partly on usefulness to school ensembles

## 10.7 Market validation plan

Before scaling beyond the first cohort, NOVA 8 will complete:

- At least 15 director or percussion-educator conversations
- At least three facility discussions
- A family interest form with preferred days, travel tolerance, price sensitivity, experience, and program interest
- One or more paid public clinics to test conversion and operations
- A founding enrollment deposit test
- Post-event surveys that distinguish enthusiasm from willingness to enroll

The strongest evidence of demand is not social-media engagement. It is direct family interest, educator referral, paid registration, attendance, completion, and renewal.

# 11. Recruitment, marketing, and enrollment

## 11.1 Access model

NOVA 8 uses open, direct enrollment for its public student programs. A director may recommend or share NOVA 8, but the student and family decide whether to apply. NOVA 8, not the school, confirms program fit, placement, aid, and enrollment.

The two recruitment channels run in parallel:

| Direct-to-family channel | Educator-supported channel |
|---|---|
| Website, public clinics, community outreach, family referrals, and word of mouth | Director or instructor shares information or recommends the program |
| Family contacts NOVA 8 directly | Family contacts NOVA 8 after hearing from an educator |
| No school involvement is required | Educator input is optional and consent-based |
| Same placement, tuition, aid, and safety standards | Same placement, tuition, aid, and safety standards |

Neither channel has priority in admission. Both lead to the same family-controlled enrollment process.

## 11.2 Positioning

### Primary message

> **NOVA 8 Percussion. More time to grow.**

### School-facing message

> NOVA 8 extends the instructional capacity of school music programs and returns stronger, better-prepared students to their ensembles.

### Family-facing message

> Serious ensemble training in an encouraging, noncompetitive environment, with instruments provided, a clear eight-week commitment, and no director referral required.

### 8th Grade Intensive message

> Enter high-school percussion ready to listen, learn, move, prepare, and contribute.

### Public-clinic message

> Step inside the ensemble. No season-long commitment required.

## 11.3 Recruitment channels

NOVA 8 should develop direct family access and educator relationships at the same time. Within those two parallel tracks, practical channels include:

1. Website interest and direct family inquiry
2. Percussion Playground and public clinics
3. Family and participant referrals
4. Educator referrals and direct relationships
5. Partner-school communication to families
6. University and independent-ensemble partners
7. Social media and community calendars
8. Paid digital promotion only when organic and partner channels are understood

## 11.4 Enrollment process

The family completes this process directly with NOVA 8. A school signature, director recommendation, or partner-school affiliation is not required.

1. Family submits an interest form
2. NOVA 8 confirms program fit and dates
3. Student completes a readiness questionnaire or placement activity
4. Family receives placement, tuition, aid, and attendance information
5. Family signs required agreements and pays a deposit or selects an aid/payment option
6. Enrollment is confirmed
7. Family attends orientation or reviews orientation materials
8. Student begins with all safety, media, medical, and release information complete

If a family wants the student's director involved, NOVA 8 may request relevant input or share progress after obtaining clear consent. Declining that communication will not disadvantage the student.

## 11.5 Admissions philosophy

NOVA 8 is placement-based rather than exclusion-based. Admission and placement decisions are made by NOVA 8 using published criteria, not by a student's school affiliation or director participation. Capacity, safety, instrumentation, and instructional fit may limit enrollment. Advanced offerings may have prerequisites, but the organization should provide a constructive next step whenever possible.

## 11.6 Deposits, refunds, and payment plans

Recommended policy:

- A modest deposit reserves the seat
- Payment plans are available without stigma
- Need-based aid may cover some or all tuition
- Full refunds are available before a published commitment date, less unavoidable processing fees if permitted
- After the commitment date, refunds depend on whether the seat can be filled and on documented exceptions
- If NOVA 8 cancels, families receive a refund or may elect a credit
- Policies are written in plain language before payment

## 11.7 Launch funnel targets

To fill a 20-student founding cohort, NOVA 8 should target:

- 15 or more educator conversations
- 8 or more educators willing to share or refer
- 30 or more leads generated through direct family and public-event channels
- 75 qualified family or student leads
- 35 completed interest forms
- 25 placement-ready applicants
- 20 confirmed students
- A waitlist of at least 5 students where instrumentation allows

These are management assumptions. Actual conversion data should replace them after the first cycle.

## 11.8 Public-event follow-up

Every clinic should include:

- A concise welcome and explanation of NOVA 8
- A strong participatory experience
- A clear next step appropriate to the participant
- Permission-based email follow-up
- A short satisfaction and interest survey
- Tracking of whether the attendee later enrolls, hosts, volunteers, or donates

## 11.9 Cooperative marketing safeguards

- Do not use a school’s name, logo, student list, or communication channel without permission
- Do not imply district endorsement unless formally authorized
- Do not compare one school program against another
- Do not target students during a conflicting school commitment
- Give partner educators advance notice of major recruitment pushes
- Describe NOVA 8 as supplemental and cooperative in all primary materials
- State clearly that families may enroll directly and that director approval is not required

# 13. Operations plan

Preferred facilities include a school gym, band hall, fieldhouse, warehouse-style arts space, or large multipurpose room with:

- Permission for percussion volume
- Safe loading and unloading
- Secure or scheduled equipment access
- Restrooms
- Accessible entrance and circulation
- Family check-in area
- Emergency exits and procedures
- Reliable calendar access
- Nearby parking
- Climate control appropriate to the season
- A written facility-use agreement

Long-term storage is desirable but not required for launch if transport is planned and insured.

## 13.2 Facility strategy

Priority order:

1. In-kind school or institutional host
2. Reduced-rate recurring educational partner
3. Commercial rental with a predictable multi-date agreement
4. Rotating event venues only for public clinics, not for the core student program

The core program should not open registration until all eight instructional dates and a contingency date are secured.

## 13.3 Instruments and equipment

NOVA Performing Arts owns the core instruments and electronics. Pre-launch work should focus on:

- Complete inventory with condition and serial information
- Head replacement plan
- Mallet, stick, and implement quantities
- Cables, power, amplification, and hearing-protection needs
- Safe transport and loading
- Storage and access controls
- Setup diagrams
- Maintenance responsibility
- Instrument assignment and check-out records

## 13.4 Staffing model

### Founding minimum

- Program or executive lead
- Lead educator or artistic director
- One or more section instructors based on enrollment
- Two to four teaching fellows
- Program administrator or assigned administrative contractor
- Screened event and check-in support

Staffing ratios should be determined by student age, facility layout, instrumentation, activity, and safety policy. A two-adult operational minimum should apply whenever youth are present, even if enrollment is small.

## 13.5 Weekly operating cycle

- Confirm staff and facility
- Review student absences and support needs
- Prepare lesson plan and equipment map
- Inspect instruments and safety conditions
- Set up check-in

- Maintain adult-boundary and communication rules

### After rehearsal

- Authorized student release
- Equipment count and condition check
- Staff debrief
- Family communication if needed
- Attendance and incident records completed
- Lesson adjustment for the next week

## 13.8 Insurance and contracts

Before launch, NOVA Performing Arts should review with qualified professionals:

# 19. Three-year growth strategy

## 19.1 Year 1: Prove usefulness

Primary objective: demonstrate that the cooperative model produces a credible student experience and value for school programs.

Priorities:

- Deliver two core cohorts
- Pilot the 8th Grade Intensive
- Establish six public social clinics and two Percussion Playground events
- Build three to five active school relationships
- Pilot the Teaching Fellowship
- Document curriculum and operations
- Establish fundraising and sponsorship language
- Finish with a positive operating result

## 19.2 Year 2: Establish predictability

Primary objective: offer a reliable annual calendar and deepen partnerships.

Priorities:

- Four core cohorts across the year
- Two 8th Grade Intensives
- Ten public clinics and four Percussion Playground events
- Six to ten active school relationships
- Four to eight fellows
- One or more institutional instructional partnerships
- At least one school or district service contract
- More recurring individual donors and sponsors
- Formal curriculum and staff-training materials

## 19.3 Year 3: Expand carefully

Primary objective: increase access without diluting instruction or cooperation.

Priorities:

- Six core cohorts, potentially across more than one readiness level or site
- Three 8th Grade Intensives
- Fourteen social clinics and six Percussion Playground events
- Ten to fifteen active school relationships
- Six to twelve fellows
- Multiple contracted or co-designed partner programs
- Stronger administrative capacity
- A reserve approaching one month of annual cash expense, with a longer-term goal of three months
- Evaluation of a long-term rehearsal home

## 19.4 Expansion tests

NOVA 8 should add a new site or cohort only when:

- Existing cohorts meet quality and completion targets
- A qualified lead educator is available
- A school or community need is documented
- The facility is reliable
- Enrollment is supported by referrals or deposits
- Safety and administrative systems can support the increase
- The expansion has a complete budget and cash plan

## 19.5 Replication principle

NOVA 8 should replicate standards and relationships, not merely a repertoire packet. Each site must preserve open student access, the school-cooperation pledge, the educational model, staff quality, safety system, evaluation, and financial discipline.

# 21. Risk register and contingencies

| Risk | Early warning | Mitigation | Decision threshold or response |
|---|---|---|---|
| Insufficient enrollment | Low qualified interest, few deposits | Educator referrals, clinic conversion, early gates | Resize at 12 to 15; delay below 12 unless fully funded pilot is approved |
| Facility instability | Dates not contracted, changing access | Written agreement, backup venue, contingency date | Do not open core registration without all dates |
| School-program conflict | Director concern, overlapping calendar | Partner pledge, conflict calendar, advance notice | Change schedule or scope before asking students to choose |
| Lead educator unavailable | Search delay or inconsistent commitment | Early contract, backup instructors, documented curriculum | Delay rather than launch without qualified leadership |
| Cash shortfall | Gifts delayed, aid exceeds budget | Founding funding gate, deposits, rolling cash forecast | Do not begin a cycle that cannot be completed with cash on hand or committed |
| Tuition unaffordability | High aid requests, abandoned applications | Aid fund, payment plans, sponsor-a-student campaign | Reprice only after analyzing demand and full cost |
| Overreliance on one donor or partner | One source dominates budget or facility | Diversify giving, contracts, and sites | Board reviews any source above 25% of annual cash revenue |
| Uneven student readiness | Wide placement spread | Readiness assessment, levels, instrumentation choices | Split or redesign cohort if one plan cannot serve students safely and well |
| Safety incident | Policy gaps, incomplete screening, near misses | Training, two-adult practices, incident system, insurance | Pause affected activity and follow reporting and response policy |
| Equipment damage or transport failure | Maintenance backlog, unclear custody | Inventory, transport plan, reserves, insurance | Modify instrumentation or cancel safely if equipment is not ready |
| Staff burnout | Repeated last-minute coverage, excessive volunteer load | Paid roles, realistic calendar, backups, workload review | Reduce activity before adding under-supported programs |
| Mission drift toward competition | Ranking language, performance pressure | Board principles, educator review, outcome measures | Remove practices that turn growth into competitive selection |
| Perception of recruiting from schools | Director concern, conflicting messaging | Transparent referral process and partner communication | Stop the disputed recruitment practice and repair the relationship |
| Public clinic loses money | Weak sales, excessive production cost | Capacity targets, sponsor, simple format, direct-cost budget | Cancel by published deadline or treat as approved cultivation expense |
| Data or privacy problem | Informal student information exchange | Consent, minimal data, role access, approved systems | Contain, document, notify, and review according to policy and law |
| Growth exceeds administration | Delayed communication, reporting errors | Capacity gates, admin staffing, documented processes | Freeze expansion until core operations stabilize |

# Conclusion

NOVA 8 has a credible opening because it begins with assets that are difficult to acquire, including the instruments, electronics, a clear educational identity, and a compelling public experience. Its success will depend on less visible assets: direct family trust, constructive school relationships, excellent educators, safe operations, dependable space, accessible pricing, and enough unrestricted support to complete every promise made to students.

The defining strategic choice is **open access with responsible cooperation**. Families may choose NOVA 8 without asking a director or waiting for a school partnership. NOVA 8, in turn, accepts the responsibility to complement school music programs rather than compete with them. It will not measure growth by how many students it can pull into a separate organization. It will measure growth by how effectively students develop and how much stronger they are when they return to the ensembles and communities that already need them.

That makes the plan both educationally useful and institutionally distinctive. Public clinics create an open door. Eight-week programs create sustained development. The 8th Grade Intensive addresses a specific transition. The Teaching Fellowship develops future educators. School partnerships hold the model together.

If NOVA 8 secures the lead educator, rehearsal site, safety infrastructure, founding funding, and a 16-to-20-student cohort by the October decision gate, it should proceed with the November 14, 2026 founding cycle. If those conditions are not met, it should resize or delay openly rather than compromise quality, safety, or its cooperative commitments.
