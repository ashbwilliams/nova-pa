# NOVA 8 Band Director and Percussion Director Pressure Test

**Prepared for:** NOVA Performing Arts  
**Date:** July 21, 2026  
**Document reviewed:** *NOVA 8 Percussion Business Plan*, July 21, 2026  
**Purpose:** Identify likely school-educator objections before partnership outreach and convert credible concerns into operating safeguards.

> This analysis intentionally adopts the perspective of a skeptical band director or percussion director. It does not assume that every objection will arise. It asks whether NOVA 8 can answer each objection with evidence, policy, or a change in design rather than reassurance alone.

---

# Executive assessment

The business plan expresses the right intention: NOVA 8 should complement school music programs and strengthen students' participation in them. A thoughtful director will recognize that intention.

The current plan does not yet fully prove the claim operationally. Its largest vulnerabilities are:

1. **The November 14 to January 30 founding calendar is presented too confidently.** It may conflict with football playoffs, concert preparation, region auditions, semester exams, holidays, indoor auditions, and indoor-season rehearsals. Avoiding the state marching contest alone does not establish compatibility with local programs.
2. **“Extending instructional capacity” can sound like a claim that schools are deficient.** It may also imply that NOVA expects directors to outsource instruction or help recruit customers.
3. **The 8th Grade Intensive could interfere with feeder-program articulation.** A high-school percussion director may object to an outside organization teaching incoming students technique, vocabulary, movement, or rehearsal expectations that differ from the receiving program.
4. **The plan asks for trust before it names the instructional authority.** Directors will want to know who teaches, whose pedagogy governs, how fellows are supervised, and whether NOVA instructors have conflicts with school employment or private teaching.
5. **A school host relationship is operationally heavier than the plan suggests.** A director often cannot independently authorize facilities, public ticketed events, insurance terms, student communications, equipment use, or data exchange.
6. **The model can create equity and access concerns.** A $495 to $525 fee, recurring family transportation, and one regional site may disproportionately serve students whose families already have time, money, and musical access.
7. **Public clinics, donor cultivation, adult events, and youth instruction could blur the mission.** Directors may worry that their students, facilities, names, or referrals will become fundraising assets for an outside organization.
8. **The financial plan depends materially on fundraising and uncommitted institutional revenue.** A partner needs assurance that a cycle will finish without a mid-session emergency appeal, price change, or request that the school absorb costs.

These concerns are manageable. None defeats the concept. They do mean that the cooperative promise should be converted into a small set of enforceable practices before NOVA asks directors to refer students, host activities, or endorse the program.

## Overall verdict

**Proceed with educator discovery and a limited founding pilot, but do not present the current calendar, partnership benefits, or eight-week model as final.** The concept is strongest when NOVA offers a carefully bounded supplemental opportunity in response to director-identified needs. It is weakest when NOVA appears to have designed a regional program first and seeks school cooperation afterward.

## Risk scale

- **Red:** Could prevent referral or partnership and requires a design decision before launch
- **Amber:** Credible concern that requires a written safeguard or evidence
- **Green:** The current plan substantially addresses the concern, although execution still matters

# 1. Core positioning under pressure

## Likely pushback

> “Why is an outside nonprofit needed? We already teach percussion. Are you implying our program cannot develop its own students?”

This is the most important positioning risk. The current school-facing phrase, “extends the instructional capacity of school music programs,” is logical from NOVA's perspective but can be heard as a judgment about a school's staffing, time, or quality. “Returns stronger, better-prepared students” can also imply that the director did not prepare them adequately.

## Assessment: Amber

The plan repeatedly calls schools the student's primary educational home and rejects replacement or competition. That is a strong foundation. The language still needs to be more deferential and more specific about who identifies the need.

## Recommended response

NOVA should not lead with a generalized “gap” in school instruction. It should lead with a narrower proposition:

> **NOVA 8 offers additional ensemble time for students who want it, reinforcing transferable skills identified in consultation with local educators. Its purpose is to support students' participation in their school programs, not to duplicate or redirect it.**

This framing:

- Does not diagnose a school as deficient
- Makes participation optional
- Gives educators a role in defining useful skills
- Avoids promising that every student will return “stronger”
- Positions the school ensemble as the student's musical home

## Language to avoid

- “We solve what schools do not have time to teach.”
- “We give students the training they are not getting at school.”
- “We return better players to your program.”
- “We can take percussion off your plate.”
- “NOVA is the next level for your strongest students.”

Even when factually intended as compliments, these phrases can sound competitive, extractive, or dismissive.

# 2. Calendar and commitment conflicts

## Likely pushback

> “You say you will avoid school conflicts, but November through January is not open. We still have football, concerts, region auditions, finals, holidays, indoor auditions, and indoor rehearsals.”

## Assessment: Red

The current plan selects November 14 because it follows the UIL state marching contest period. That is not enough to establish a cooperative calendar. School commitments do not end with state marching contest dates, and a regional cohort may involve schools with very different football, concert, audition, and indoor schedules.

The eight instructional dates are especially vulnerable:

| Proposed date | Potential director concern |
|---|---|
| November 14 | Football playoffs, post-marching recovery, all-region preparation, indoor auditions |
| November 21 | Thanksgiving travel, football playoffs, concert preparation |
| December 5 and 12 | Region auditions, concerts, semester-end academic demands |
| December 19 | Family travel, school closure, low attendance reliability |
| January 9 and 16 | Region events, indoor rehearsals, winterguard or percussion schedules, MLK weekend |
| January 23 and 30 | Indoor season, solo and ensemble preparation, school events |

There may be no universally conflict-free eight-week season. The solution is not a broader promise to avoid every conflict. The solution is to define a target student segment and partner group, collect their calendars, and choose the least harmful window with explicit attendance expectations.

## Required change

Replace the fixed founding date with a **provisional launch window and a director-validation gate**.

Recommended gate:

- At least three anchor directors representing the intended student population review the complete proposed calendar
- No anchor director identifies a recurring required conflict for the students NOVA expects to serve
- NOVA documents which conflicts remain and explains them to families before deposits
- A student is never encouraged to miss a school rehearsal, performance, audition, or required activity for NOVA
- Refund and make-up policies do not punish students for newly scheduled school obligations

## Better founding options

NOVA should test these with directors rather than choosing one internally:

1. **November public clinic followed by a later eight-week cycle.** This preserves a November public launch while reducing the risk of an unvalidated full-cycle calendar.
2. **Short founding pilot.** Three or four sessions can test instruction, transport, attendance, and cooperation before a full cycle.
3. **Partner-specific cohort.** A cohort drawn from a small group of aligned feeder programs is easier to schedule than a region-wide open cohort.
4. **Age-specific calendar.** Middle-school students, high-school students, and indoor participants have different constraints and should not automatically share one schedule.

## Important revision to the partnership pledge

The promise to “avoid conflicts” is too absolute. NOVA cannot control changing school calendars across Central Texas. A more credible promise is:

> **NOVA 8 will plan around known school commitments, never ask a student to choose NOVA over a required school activity, and revise or excuse participation when unavoidable conflicts arise.**

# 3. 8th Grade Intensive and feeder-program alignment

## Likely pushback

> “Incoming eighth graders need to learn our approach. If NOVA teaches a different grip, set position, vocabulary, movement system, or audition expectation, I will spend the summer undoing it.”

## Assessment: Red

The 8th Grade Intensive is one of NOVA's clearest potential benefits, but it is also the program most likely to cross an instructional boundary. High-school programs have legitimate differences in technique, pedagogy, instrumentation, audition processes, and culture. “Transferable fundamentals” is not self-defining.

## Required design principle

The Intensive should not be marketed as generic preparation for “high-school percussion” until NOVA has established how it handles program-specific differences.

Recommended model:

- Organize the Intensive with one or more receiving high-school programs, feeder clusters, or an advisory group
- Ask receiving directors to define a short list of desired readiness outcomes
- Separate universal outcomes from program-specific practices
- Disclose any material technique differences to students and directors
- Do not teach receiving-program audition material, packet content, exercises, or show music without written authorization
- Do not predict placement or tell students which school instrument they “should” receive
- Emphasize reading, pulse, listening, preparation, equipment care, movement awareness, and rehearsal habits when technique systems differ

## Universal versus director-specific content

| Lower-risk transferable content | Content requiring director alignment |
|---|---|
| Pulse, subdivision, listening | Grip and fulcrum terminology |
| Reading confidence | Set position and playing zones |
| Practice habits | Stroke taxonomy and technique sequence |
| Equipment care and safe movement | Marching style and visual vocabulary |
| Rehearsal etiquette | Audition material and placement expectations |
| Asking for clarification | School-specific exercises, packets, or show content |

## Recommended rename if alignment is not secured

If NOVA cannot establish receiving-program collaboration, consider calling the initial offering an **8th Grade Percussion Readiness Lab** rather than an “Intensive.” “Readiness” better supports a fundamentals-first scope and makes fewer claims about high-school preparation.

# 4. Conflicting technique and instructional authority

## Likely pushback

> “Whose technique are you teaching, and what happens when it conflicts with mine?”

## Assessment: Red until the lead educator and curriculum authority are defined

The plan has good curriculum domains, but it does not yet identify who owns pedagogical decisions or how differences with school programs are handled. An advisory group can provide input, but a committee cannot resolve every rehearsal decision.

## Required safeguards

- Name a qualified lead educator before recruitment begins
- Publish a concise instructional philosophy and sample curriculum
- Identify the lead educator as the final authority within NOVA activities
- Create a director-alignment intake for partner cohorts
- Document known areas where established programs differ
- Train all section staff and fellows to avoid saying that one legitimate school method is “wrong”
- Give students language for switching appropriately between systems
- Establish a process for a director to raise a concern and receive a timely response

## Credible answer to a director

> “NOVA has a defined instructional approach, but we do not treat local differences as defects. Before a partner cohort begins, we ask which practices matter for transfer. We concentrate on shared fundamentals, disclose meaningful differences, and never tell a student to disregard the expectations of the student's school program.”

# 5. Recruitment, loyalty, and perceived poaching

## Likely pushback

> “Are you using my students and parent list to build your organization? Will you recruit our strongest players into NOVA instead of reinforcing their commitment here?”

## Assessment: Amber to Red

The business plan already includes transparent recruitment safeguards. The remaining risk comes from the business model itself. NOVA needs tuition, event attendance, referrals, sponsors, and donor cultivation. A director may reasonably wonder whether the school relationship is educational or primarily a customer-acquisition channel.

## Required safeguards

- Obtain permission before using a school name, logo, communication channel, or on-campus access
- Never request a student list from a director
- Let families initiate contact unless a formal, consented referral process exists
- State in the family agreement that required school commitments take priority
- Do not advertise NOVA placement, titles, or instruments as superior to school assignments
- Do not use partner-school students in fundraising stories without separate informed consent
- Do not solicit donations from families through a director's channel unless the school explicitly approves it
- Do not compensate educators for referrals
- Disclose any NOVA instructor's employment, private-lesson, or ensemble recruiting conflicts
- Do not use a NOVA event to recruit for another independent or competitive ensemble

## Important boundary

“Returning student, family supporter, volunteer, or host” is a reasonable internal pathway, but it should not appear to educators as the primary reason for a public clinic. School-facing materials should clearly separate educational participation from donor cultivation.

# 6. Student workload, burnout, and divided attention

## Likely pushback

> “My students already have band, academics, lessons, region music, indoor, sports, jobs, and family responsibilities. Why should I recommend another commitment?”

## Assessment: Amber

An eight-week commitment is shorter than a competitive season, but weekly rehearsal, travel, practice, setup, and a culminating event still create meaningful load. “Noncompetitive” does not mean low-burden.

## Recommended safeguards

- Publish the total expected time, including home practice and setup
- Keep home assignments limited and purposeful
- Ask about school, academic, and family conflicts before placement
- Require families to acknowledge that school obligations take priority
- Avoid attendance incentives that punish excused school conflicts
- Do not create a culture in which students must choose the most advanced or prestigious activity
- Monitor student workload at mid-cycle
- Give directors an easy way to flag a sudden conflict without negotiating with multiple NOVA staff

NOVA's strongest answer is not that students “need more.” It is that the program offers a bounded, optional experience with a clear beginning and end, and it will decline or defer enrollment when the additional commitment is not healthy.

---

# 7. Teaching Fellowship credibility

## Likely pushback

> “Are college students going to practice teaching on my students? Are fellows being used as cheap labor?”

## Assessment: Amber

The plan correctly states that fellows are supervised and should not replace professional instruction. Directors will still want concrete staffing rules.

## Required safeguards

- The lead educator is present and instructionally responsible at every youth rehearsal
- Fellows do not conduct unsupervised one-to-one instruction with minors
- Fellows never serve as the only qualified adult for a section or cohort
- Fellowship roles, screening, training, observation, and evaluation are written
- Families know which staff are lead educators, section staff, and fellows
- Staffing ratios are based on student age, instrumentation, facility layout, and activity risk
- The program does not count an untrained volunteer as instructional coverage
- A partner institution's referral is not treated as proof of youth-teaching readiness

## Stronger positioning

The fellowship should be described as a **secondary educational purpose with its own quality controls**, not as part of the reason student tuition is affordable. The student experience must remain defensible even if no institutional fellowship partner is available.

# 8. Staff qualifications and conflicts of interest

## Likely pushback

> “Who are the instructors, what have they taught, and are they permitted to do this under their district or university policies?”

## Assessment: Red before enrollment

The program cannot borrow credibility from the general idea of “experienced educators.” Directors will evaluate the named people.

## Pre-launch evidence packet

NOVA should be ready to provide:

- Lead educator biography and relevant youth-teaching experience
- Roles and supervision chart
- Background-screening standard
- Youth-protection training requirements
- Staff code of conduct
- Proof of insurance upon appropriate request
- Conflict-of-interest disclosure process
- Policy on instructors recruiting private students or outside-ensemble members
- Complaint and escalation contact

Employees of schools, districts, universities, or independent ensembles may have outside-employment, facility-use, intellectual-property, or recruiting restrictions. NOVA should require disclosure and written confirmation that each instructor is responsible for complying with those obligations.

# 9. School facility burden

## Likely pushback

> “Providing a gym is not a simple in-kind contribution. Who handles district approval, custodial fees, keys, security, visitors, damage, storage, and insurance?”

## Assessment: Red for any school-hosted program

The current facility section lists physical needs well but understates governance. A director may be an advocate without having authority to approve use.

## Required host model

NOVA should assume that a school-hosted activity may require approval from some combination of:

- Campus administration
- District fine arts leadership
- Facilities or community-use office
- Risk management
- Security
- Custodial services
- Transportation or loading personnel
- Booster organization, if involved

## Written agreement should allocate

- Dates, hours, access, and cancellation authority
- Public versus student-only access
- Check-in and visitor control
- Custodial, utility, security, and staffing fees
- Certificate of insurance and additional-insured requirements
- Indemnity and damage responsibility, reviewed by appropriate counsel
- Equipment ownership, use, storage, and repair
- Loading routes and vehicle access
- Emergency procedures and incident notification
- Accessibility
- Media, signage, ticketing, concessions, and fundraising permissions
- School name and logo use

## Important conclusion

Ticketed public or adult clinics on a school campus are materially different from a closed youth rehearsal. NOVA should not assume that a school willing to host one will host the other. Public clinics may need a separate community venue.

# 10. Liability, youth protection, and institutional trust

## Likely pushback

> “If something happens, whose policy applies and who is responsible?”

## Assessment: Amber, with strong foundations in the plan

The plan addresses screening, communication, hearing safety, transport, incident response, and two-adult practices. That is stronger than many early-stage program concepts. The remaining issue is verification and compatibility with partner requirements.

## Required safeguards

- Adopt policies before enrollment, not during the first cycle
- Confirm background-check criteria, rescreening frequency, and disqualifying findings
- Define mandatory-reporting training and escalation
- Specify adult-to-student ratios and one-to-one interaction rules
- Establish check-in, authorized release, late pickup, and restroom procedures
- Document hearing-exposure controls, safe lifting, and instrument fitting
- Maintain emergency contacts, medical information, incident forms, and trained responders
- Clarify whether school policies supplement or supersede NOVA rules on a host campus
- Require written facility and program agreements rather than relying on a director's informal approval
- Have insurance and legal professionals review the model before representing it as complete

NOVA should avoid saying “we follow school policy” unless the applicable policy has been identified and incorporated. Different districts and facilities may impose different requirements.

# 11. Student data, referrals, and progress reports

## Likely pushback

> “I cannot casually share student information with you, and I do not have time to complete outside evaluations.”

## Assessment: Amber

The plan appropriately requires family consent and prefers program-level feedback. It should go further by designing the relationship so that no director must disclose identifiable student information for NOVA to function.

## Recommended model

- Directors share a public program link or paper information rather than student names
- Families initiate applications
- Any named referral uses a written, consented process
- NOVA collects only the information needed for enrollment, safety, aid, and instruction
- Student summaries go to families first
- Families may authorize transmission to a school educator
- Partner evaluation uses a two-minute rubric or short annual conversation
- NOVA does not ask a school to place its internal records in NOVA systems
- Data-retention and deletion periods are defined

“Director feedback” should be optional unless it is part of a formal institutional agreement. A cooperative model should reduce, not create, uncompensated administrative work.

# 12. Equity, tuition, and transportation

## Likely pushback

> “The students who most need this may be the least able to pay $495, travel 30 to 45 minutes each week, or arrive with family support.”

## Assessment: Red to mission credibility

The plan's scholarship commitment is good, but a 15 percent aggregate aid assumption may not match the population NOVA says it wants to reach. Transportation is assigned to families at launch, which is operationally sensible but creates a selection effect.

## Required validation

- Ask families about price and travel before finalizing the site
- Ask directors whether the proposed fee makes them reluctant to refer students
- Track applicants who do not complete enrollment and why
- Show aid availability at the first point where price appears
- Reserve aid before recruitment, rather than depending on later fundraising
- Offer full as well as partial awards
- Consider partner-school reserved seats or sponsor-underwritten cohorts
- Evaluate carpools only through a safe, family-directed model unless NOVA later adopts a transportation policy
- Avoid attendance or payment policies that disproportionately penalize families with variable work and transport schedules

## Financial stress test

If the founding cohort receives more aid than the assumed 15 percent, the cycle must still be able to finish. Before opening enrollment, NOVA should model at least:

- 15 percent tuition aid
- 30 percent tuition aid
- 50 percent tuition aid
- 12, 16, and 20 students at each aid level

The board should know which scenarios are mission-successful and financially survivable.

# 13. Mixed readiness, placement, and school roles

## Likely pushback

> “What happens when your placement differs from our placement, or a student thinks a NOVA role entitles them to that role at school?”

## Assessment: Amber

NOVA's placement-based philosophy is reasonable. The plan needs an explicit separation between NOVA assignments and school assignments.

## Required safeguards

- State that NOVA placement applies only to NOVA activities
- Do not predict or comment on a student's likely school placement
- Explain that cross-training may be educational rather than hierarchical
- Avoid “top,” “elite,” or status-based labels
- Make instrumentation decisions based on learning goals, balance, safety, and equipment
- Give families a process to discuss placement without involving school staff in disputes
- Do not ask directors to justify their school assignments to NOVA students or parents

For mixed-readiness cohorts, the lead educator should demonstrate how a single rehearsal plan can challenge advanced students without leaving beginners behind. If that cannot be done, the cohort should be split or narrowed.

# 14. Noncompetitive rigor and performance value

## Likely pushback

> “Without auditions, competition, or a major performance, will students take it seriously? What is the instructional outcome?”

## Assessment: Amber

Noncompetitive is a meaningful distinction, but it should not become vague. Directors may support the philosophy while still expecting measurable musicianship and dependable rehearsal behavior.

## Recommended evidence of rigor

- Published eight-week learning objectives
- Baseline and end-of-cycle performance tasks
- Attendance and preparation expectations
- A musically credible culminating demonstration
- Repertoire selected for instructional value, not spectacle
- Short student reflection and instructor summary
- Aggregate outcome reporting without inflated claims

NOVA should say **noncompetitive, not non-evaluative**. Students can receive clear feedback and meet high standards without rankings or contest scores.

---

# 15. Public clinics and Percussion Playground

## Likely pushback

> “How do ticketed adult or donor experiences help students, and are you using a school facility or our program's identity to raise money?”

## Assessment: Amber

Public participation can be a distinctive mission activity and an earned-revenue source. It can also look disconnected from school partnership if not carefully separated.

## Required boundaries

- Give every public event a stated educational, access, or cultivation purpose
- Track direct cost and contribution separately from youth instruction
- Do not imply that an event is school-sponsored without formal approval
- Do not use students as performers or demonstrations for donors without appropriate consent and educational purpose
- Do not charge families simply to observe their own child's culminating program event
- Consider whether observer tickets create an unnecessary access barrier
- Keep adult public access separate from routine youth rehearsals
- Use separate venue, staffing, check-in, and insurance assumptions when necessary
- Report how event proceeds support the mission when cross-subsidy is claimed

## Specific concern about observer fees

Charging $10 to $15 for observers may be acceptable for a standalone public experience, but it could create poor optics if applied to parents watching a youth activity or culminating demonstration. The plan should distinguish public-event observers from immediate family access.

# 16. Sustainability and financial credibility

## Likely pushback

> “Will this still exist after the founding grant? Are you expecting the school or booster organization to cover a shortfall?”

## Assessment: Amber to Red

The plan is admirably transparent that tuition does not cover full program cost. It also assumes $85,000 in Year 1 contributed revenue and a shift from 32 percent to 53 percent earned revenue by Year 3. Those assumptions need evidence.

Potential director concerns include:

- A program begins but cannot complete the cycle
- A school partner is asked to rescue an underfunded cohort
- Tuition rises sharply after the pilot
- The director becomes an informal fundraiser
- “School and institutional contracts” are counted before any purchasing pathway or commitment exists
- Staff compensation is too low to retain the promised instructional quality

## Required safeguards

- Hold sufficient unrestricted or contractually committed cash to complete each cycle before it begins
- Tell partners that referral does not create a financial obligation
- Do not include school contract revenue in the launch base case until a contract or credible procurement pathway exists
- Show a budget without in-kind space as well as a budget with it
- Publish cancellation and refund protections
- Keep director fundraising optional and separate from instructional cooperation
- Review whether the Year 2 and Year 3 growth rates require staffing and administrative capacity not represented in the plan

The strongest financial trust signal is simple: **once a cycle accepts students, NOVA can complete it without additional fundraising, enrollment, or school subsidy.**

# 17. Governance and who can speak for a school

## Likely pushback

> “I like the idea, but I cannot commit the campus or district.”

## Assessment: Amber

The partnership levels are helpful, but the plan sometimes treats “the school” or “the director” as a single decision-maker. Authority varies.

## Recommended stakeholder map

| Decision | Likely school-side authority or stakeholder |
|---|---|
| General educator advice | Band director or percussion director |
| Referral or flyer sharing | Director, campus administration, or district communications policy |
| Facility use | Campus administration and district facilities or community-use office |
| District endorsement | Fine arts administration or authorized district leadership |
| Student data exchange | Family consent plus authorized school personnel and policy review |
| Equipment use | Director plus campus or district property authority |
| Contracted services | District purchasing and authorized signatory |
| Public ticketed event | Facilities, administration, risk, security, and finance stakeholders |

NOVA should never put a supportive director in the position of appearing to promise authority the director does not have. The first conversation should ask, “If this were useful, who else would need to review it?”

# 18. Relationship with existing percussion staff and private instructors

## Likely pushback

> “We already have percussion staff and private teachers. Does NOVA compete with their work or create employment conflicts?”

## Assessment: Amber

The competitive analysis calls private lessons complementary, but the operating plan should recognize that local educators may see overlapping instruction, recruitment, and paid opportunities.

## Recommended safeguards

- Invite current program staff into discovery when the band director requests it
- Do not imply that NOVA instructors supersede campus percussion staff
- Establish a conflict policy for private lesson solicitation
- Prohibit staff from using NOVA student data for personal business
- Disclose when a NOVA instructor also teaches at a partner school or competing ensemble
- Avoid scheduling that forces local percussion instructors to choose between school duties and NOVA duties
- Consider paid consulting or clinic roles when a partner director contributes substantial curriculum work, subject to district policy

# 19. Repertoire, intellectual property, and school materials

## Likely pushback

> “Will you use our exercises, audition packet, arrangements, show music, recordings, or branding?”

## Assessment: Amber

The plan says school-specific show material will not be taught without authorization. The same boundary should explicitly cover all school-owned or licensed materials.

## Required safeguards

- Use original, properly licensed, public-domain, or otherwise authorized repertoire
- Do not copy school audition packets, exercises, arrangements, recordings, drill, or show materials without permission
- Clarify ownership of curriculum created by employees, contractors, fellows, and institutional partners
- Obtain permission before recording or sharing a partner-hosted activity
- Keep school logos and names out of promotion until approved

# 20. Claims, evidence, and reputational risk

## Likely pushback

> “What evidence do you have that students return more capable, and are you using my endorsement to validate an unproven model?”

## Assessment: Amber

The plan correctly treats the founding year as a pilot. Some of its headline language still reads as an established outcome rather than a hypothesis.

## Recommended claim discipline

Before outcome data exist, say:

- “Designed to reinforce...”
- “Intended to help students...”
- “NOVA will evaluate whether...”
- “In consultation with participating educators...”

Avoid saying:

- “Returns stronger students” as a guaranteed outcome
- “Builds better school ensembles” without evidence
- “Meets a regional need” before director and family validation
- “Partner school” when a director has only had an exploratory conversation
- “District-supported” without formal authorization

Director quotations, logos, and endorsements should require specific permission and should not imply broader institutional approval.

# Structural changes recommended for the business plan

The pressure test supports the overall business model but suggests nine changes before the plan is treated as externally ready.

## 1. Change the fixed November start to a gated launch window

Keep November 2026 as an organizational target, but define whether it means:

- First public clinic
- Short founding pilot
- Orientation and placement
- Full eight-week cycle only if director validation gates are met

Do not call November 14 the recommended full-cycle start until target partners have reviewed the complete calendar.

## 2. Revise the central school-facing promise

Recommended version:

> **NOVA 8 offers additional ensemble time in consultation with local educators, reinforcing transferable skills and supporting each student's participation in the student's school music program.**

This is more credible than promising to “return stronger” students before outcomes have been measured.

## 3. Add a formal non-displacement standard

NOVA should adopt a short policy stating that it will not:

- Ask students to miss required school activities
- Recruit students away from school ensembles
- Use school contact information without authorization
- Predict or challenge school placements
- Teach or use protected school materials without permission
- Solicit private students or outside-ensemble members through NOVA access

## 4. Add a director-alignment protocol

Before a partner cohort begins, document:

- Target students
- Desired transferable outcomes
- Known technique differences
- Calendar exclusions
- School-material boundaries
- Communication roles
- Evaluation method

## 5. Separate types of partnership authority

Distinguish educator support, referral permission, facility approval, contractual authority, data exchange, and institutional endorsement. Do not use “partner” as a single undifferentiated status.

## 6. Separate youth programs from public-event operations

Use different assumptions for venue access, ticketing, observers, adult participation, fundraising, insurance, and student visibility. A school may support youth programming while declining public ticketed events.

## 7. Add downside financial scenarios

At minimum, model enrollment at 12, 16, and 20 students with aid at 15, 30, and 50 percent. Remove uncommitted school contract revenue from the launch base case.

## 8. Add an external trust packet

Before asking for referrals, provide a concise packet containing:

- One-page program description
- Named instructional leadership
- Sample curriculum and calendar
- School-priority and non-displacement commitments
- Tuition and aid
- Youth-protection overview
- Insurance status
- Contact and concern-escalation pathway

## 9. Make the first cohort explicitly experimental

The founding cohort should be described as a jointly evaluated pilot. NOVA should invite critique, limit claims, and reserve the right to change the calendar, curriculum, pricing, or cohort structure based on the results.

# Director-facing questions NOVA must be able to answer

Before outreach moves from discovery to recruitment, NOVA leadership should be able to answer these questions in plain language:

1. Who is the lead educator, and what relevant youth-teaching experience does that person have?
2. Which students is the first cohort designed for?
3. What exact dates and total weekly commitment are proposed?
4. What happens when a school schedules a conflicting required event?
5. What curriculum is universal, and what content is aligned with receiving programs?
6. How will NOVA handle a technique difference with my program?
7. What will teaching fellows be permitted to do?
8. How are staff screened, trained, supervised, and insured?
9. What does a referral require from me?
10. Will NOVA ever ask me to share student names or parent contacts?
11. How are tuition and aid presented to families?
12. What prevents NOVA from recruiting students into other programs or private lessons?
13. How are NOVA assignments separated from school placements?
14. What information, if any, will I receive about a participating student?
15. What does hosting require from the campus or district?
16. Can a school cooperate without providing money, equipment, space, data, or endorsement?
17. How does a public ticketed clinic differ from the youth program?
18. What happens if enrollment or fundraising falls short?
19. How will NOVA measure whether the program was useful to schools?
20. How can I raise a concern, and who has authority to correct it?

If any answer depends on a policy that has not yet been written or a person who has not yet been selected, NOVA should say so during discovery rather than imply that the matter is settled.

# Recommended director interview sequence

The first educator conversations should not be sales calls. They should test the premises above.

## Opening

> “We are designing NOVA 8 as a supplemental, noncompetitive percussion experience. Before we settle the program, we want to understand what would make it genuinely useful to school programs and what would make you reluctant to recommend it.”

## High-value questions

1. What is your immediate concern when an outside percussion program approaches your students?
2. Which parts of the NOVA 8 concept sound useful, and which sound duplicative?
3. Which student population, if any, could benefit?
4. What dates or seasons are unrealistic for that population?
5. Which technique or curriculum differences would create problems?
6. Would an 8th Grade Intensive help or interfere with your feeder alignment?
7. What information would you need before sharing the opportunity with families?
8. What should NOVA never ask a director or school to do?
9. What evidence after one cycle would make you recommend it again?
10. Who else would need to review a referral, host, or contractual relationship?

## Evidence threshold

NOVA should not interpret politeness as demand. Record separately whether a director is:

- Willing to continue advising
- Willing to share information with families
- Willing to refer specific types of students through an approved process
- Willing to host or help seek facility approval
- Willing to provide a written or public endorsement

These are different levels of commitment.

---

# Non-negotiable protections before a founding youth cohort

NOVA should not enroll the first youth cohort until all of the following are true:

- A named qualified lead educator has accepted responsibility
- The target age and readiness range are defined
- The full calendar has been reviewed by representative anchor directors
- The facility is approved and contracted for every date
- The curriculum and director-alignment process are documented
- Staff and fellow roles, ratios, screening, and supervision are defined
- Youth-protection, emergency, check-in, release, communication, and incident policies are active
- Insurance is active and compatible with the facility agreement
- Tuition, aid, refunds, attendance, and school-conflict policies are published
- Sufficient cash is available or committed to complete the cycle
- School referrals can occur without directors transferring student data
- NOVA placement is explicitly separated from school placement
- Staff non-solicitation and conflict-of-interest rules are active
- School names, logos, materials, and endorsements are protected
- A complaint and relationship-repair process exists

# Final conclusion

The school-cooperative premise survives the pressure test, but only if it governs decisions that are inconvenient for NOVA as well as decisions that are easy to describe in the plan.

The most revealing test is this:

> **When enrollment, revenue, visibility, or a preferred date conflicts with a school program's legitimate needs, will NOVA change its plan?**

If the answer is demonstrably yes, directors may view NOVA 8 as a trusted supplemental resource. If the answer is only rhetorical, the program risks being perceived as another outside organization seeking access to students, facilities, and credibility.

The immediate priority should be a structured round of director discovery, followed by revision of the founding calendar, instructional authority, 8th Grade Intensive boundaries, host requirements, access model, and financial downside cases. With those changes, NOVA 8 can enter partnership conversations with a much stronger and more defensible proposition.
